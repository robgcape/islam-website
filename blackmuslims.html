African-American Muslims

A legacy of resilience, leadership, and spiritual transformation.

Early African Muslim Presence

Long before the Civil Rights Movement, African Muslims were brought to America through the transatlantic slave trade. Many were scholars, imams, and community leaders in West Africa. Despite the brutality of slavery, some maintained elements of their faith, leaving behind writings, oral histories, and cultural traditions.

The Rise of Black Muslim Movements

In the 20th century, African-American Muslims played a major role in shaping Islam in the U.S. Organizations like the Moorish Science Temple and the Nation of Islam provided spiritual identity, community structure, and empowerment during a time of racial oppression.

Key Figures & Movements

Nation of Islam

Founded in 1930, the Nation of Islam emphasized Black pride, economic independence, and community uplift. It played a major role in the early development of Black Muslim identity.

Malcolm X

Malcolm X transformed from a Nation of Islam minister into a global human rights leader. After performing the Hajj, he embraced Sunni Islam and advocated for universal justice.

Modern Influence

African-American Muslims continue to lead in activism, scholarship, community organizing, and interfaith work across the United States.

Contemporary African-American Muslim Life

Today, African-American Muslims make up a significant portion of the U.S. Muslim population. They contribute to arts, politics, education, social justice, and religious leadership.

Their legacy continues to shape the identity of Islam in America, bridging history, culture, and faith in powerful ways.
