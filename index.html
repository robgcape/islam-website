About Islam in America

Islam has been present in America since the 1600s, brought by enslaved West African Muslims who carried their faith, culture, and scholarship across the Atlantic. Despite forced conversion and cultural erasure, many maintained elements of Islamic identity.

Today, Islam is one of the fastest-growing religions in the United States. American Muslims contribute to education, civil rights, science, art, and community leadership across the nation.

Beliefs & Practices

Islam is built upon the Five Pillars:

  • Shahada: Declaration of faith.
  • Salah: Five daily prayers.
  • Zakat: Charity to support those in need.
  • Sawm: Fasting during Ramadan.
  • Hajj: Pilgrimage to Mecca.

These pillars guide a Muslim’s spiritual life, discipline, and connection to God and community.

The Qur'an

The Qur'an is the central religious text of Islam, believed to be the literal word of God revealed to the Prophet Muhammad over 23 years. It contains guidance on faith, morality, law, and personal conduct.

The Qur'an is divided into 114 chapters (Surahs) and is recited in Arabic. Millions of Muslims around the world memorize it in full or in part.

Islam in America

Islam in America has deep historical roots. Enslaved African Muslims were among the earliest Muslims in the country, and their legacy continues through modern African-American Muslim communities.

Today, American Muslims come from diverse backgrounds—African-American, Arab, South Asian, West African, Latino, and more—forming a vibrant and growing part of the nation’s identity.

African-American Muslims

African-American Muslims have shaped the religious and cultural landscape of the United States. Figures like Malcolm X, Muhammad Ali, and Imam Warith Deen Mohammed played major roles in civil rights, spiritual reform, and community empowerment.

Today, African-American Muslims continue to lead in activism, scholarship, and community development, preserving a legacy of resilience and faith.
